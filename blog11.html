Mood disorder

Mood is described as an internal feeling that affects almost every element of a person's behavior. It is a temporary state of mind. Affective disorders, often known as mood disorders. These are prevalent mental diseases that result in higher morbidity and death rates. Mood disorders are changes or abnormalities in the mood that affects the person’s ability to lead their life. Mood disorder involves a persistent feeling of either extreme sadness or extreme happiness. (Severe lows called depression or highs called hypomania or mania). According to DSM –5, mood disorders are broadly classified as bipolar disorder and depressive disorders.

 

Depression
There is a difference between having depression and feeling depressed. Everyone feels down from time to time. But medical depression is different, it lasts for at least two consecutive weeks.
Major depressive disorder
Depression can have lots of symptoms includes a persistent depressed mood (low mood), loss of interest in things that you normally enjoy (anhedonia), changes in appetite, feeling worthless or excessively guilty, poor concentration, Weight gain or loss, Insomnia (sleeping too little) or hypersomnia (sleeping more than 10 hours but feel unrested), restlessness or slowness, loss of energy, recurrent thoughts of suicide. The presence of 5 of the 9 symptoms of is used to diagnose. And these symptoms must last for at least two weeks. There are several other depressive disorders which are incorporated in DSM –5


Disruptive mood dysregulation disorder (DMDD)

Disruptive mood dysregulation disorder (DMDD) is a mental disorder in children and adolescents marked by a persistently irritable or angry mood and frequent temper outbursts that are out of proportion to the situation and significantly more severe than the typical reaction of their peers of the same age.

Persistent depressive disorder (PDD)

Persistent depressive disorder (PDD) or dysthymia is a long-term form of depression that last for at least two years in adults and one year in children and adolescents which is less severe than Major depressive disorder.

premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD)

The premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD) undergoes rapid changes in the mood, Symptoms usually appear in the last week before menstruation. Symptoms are mood swings, sudden sadness, highly irritable or angry resulting in increased conflicts with family and friends, hopelessness, anxious, difficulty in concentrating on everyday activities. The disorder begin to improve in a few days following the onset of menstruation, and gradually disappear in the following weeks.

Double depression

The coexistence of major depressive disorder and persistent depressive disorder is referred to as double depression. According to research, double depression is more severe than either MDD or PDD alone, and people with double depression relapse more frequently than people with either MDD or PDD alone.

Bipolar

In old literature it is called as manic depression. Bipolar I Characterized by episodes of severe mania symptoms (elevated mood with three or more of the following symptoms: increased goal-directed activity, grandiosity, a diminished need for sleep, distractibility, racing thoughts, increased/pressured speech, and reckless behaviors) has occurred for at least one week or required hospitalization Four or more of the above-mentioned symptoms are required to fulfill the fulfilled for a manic episode if the mood is irritated rather than elevated. Bipolar II Characterized by episodes of hypomania (not requiring hospitalization) and severe depression for at least 4 weeks.

Cyclothymia

Cyclothymia is characterized by the episode hypomania as well as mild depression for at least two years. These symptoms are not sufficient for a major depressive episode or a hypomanic episode. people with cyclothymia experience persistent irregular mood swings ranging from mild to moderate emotional "highs" to mild to moderate emotional "lows."

Hypomania

Hypomania is a psychiatric condition characterized by an elevated or agitated mood and is frequently occurs in persons with bipolar illness. Have less severe symptoms that are not serious enough to be hospitalized.


What Causes mood disorder?


Biological Factors

serotonin and norepinephrine are the Neurotransmitters which play an important role in the mood disorder. The chemical serotonin is most typically linked to depression. Dopamine has also been linked to mood disorders, with studies indicating that it is lower in depression and higher in mania.

Medical conditions

Medical conditions which include: Brain tumor syphilis, Delirium, Encephalitis, Influenza, Metabolic changes associated with hemodialysis, Multiple sclerosis, Q fever, Cancer, AIDS, Hypothyroidism can lead to mood disorders.

Genetic Factors

People from a family with a significant positive history of mood disorders are more likely to acquire themselves

Psychosocial Factors

Stressful life experiences (death of a significant other, parents, siblings, etc. ), traumatic occurrences, and childhood maltreatment have all been linked to the development of mood disorders later on in life, particularly depression. Personality characteristics and illnesses such as borderline and obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) are more commonly linked to depression. Depression has been linked to attachment issues and early childhood suffering


Treatment & Medications


Pharmacotherapy for Bipolar Disorder

Lithium (primary mood-stabilizer), Sodium valproate, Carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine anticonvulsant medications. ) Lamotrigine, Benzodiazepines, Thyroid hormone (In certain bipolar individuals, triiodothyronine may help to regulate their mood) are used.

Pharmacotherapy for Unipolar depression or dysthymia:


• Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs):Sertraline, fluvoxamine, fluoxetine, citalopram, escitalopram, and paroxetine.
• Serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRI):venlafaxine, desvenlafaxine, duloxetine, milnacipran, and Levo milnacipran
• Atypical antidepressantsbupropion and mirtazapine. Agomelatine is another atypical antidepressant, but it is unavailable in the United States
• Tricyclic antidepressants (TCA):desipramine, nortriptyline, imipramine, and amitriptyline.
• Serotonin modulators: s>nefazodone, trazodone, vilazodone, and vortioxetine.
• Monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOIs):>These include tranylcypromine, phenelzine, and selegiline.


Psychotherapy (talk therapy)

• Acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT)and mindfulness-based cognitive therapy (MBCT) appear to be effective in reducing mood symptoms and preventing relapse.
• Interpersonal psychotherapy is a type of psychotherapy that acknowledges interpersonal issues and role shifts.
• Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT)is a type of treatment that aims to alter the dysfunctional thoughts and attitudes that are linked to depression. CBT is the most widely     researched psychotherapy for depression among psychotherapies
• Dialectical behavioral therapy (DBT) is a kind of cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) that focuses on mindfulness, distress tolerance, and emotional regulation.
• Brain Stimulation Therapy Repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) and Electroconvulsive therapy

Phototherapy(bright light therapy) is used in the treatment of seasonal affective disorder (SAD).

Maintaining a healthy lifestyle. Regular exercise and yoga are protective against the development of mood disorders.
